Who did Evan Ellingson play on General Hospital?

 Posted on August 19, 2024  |  2 minutes  |  302 words  |  Larita Shotwell

In a recent update, the San Bernardino County coroner’s office has disclosed that the untimely death of former “CSI: Miami” actor Evan Ellingson, which occurred earlier this month, resulted from an accidental overdose of fentanyl. The coroner’s death register officially categorizes his passing as an accident.

The 35-year-old actor was discovered lifeless in his Fontana, California bedroom on November 5. Shockingly, the cause of death points to a tragic encounter with the potent opioid, fentanyl. Michael Ellingson, Evan’s father, expressed profound grief, revealing that while Evan had battled drug addiction for years, he seemed to be making positive strides recently. The sudden loss has left the family in utter devastation.

Evan Ellingson gained recognition for his role as Kyle Harmon, the son of Detective Horatio Caine, portrayed by David Caruso, in the popular television series “CSI: Miami.” His involvement spanned 18 episodes, with his last credited appearance dating back to 2010.

Beyond “CSI: Miami,” the California-born actor left his mark on notable projects throughout his career. In 2009, he shared the screen with Cameron Diaz, Alec Baldwin, and Abigail Breslin in the drama “My Sister’s Keeper” and had a memorable appearance in Clint Eastwood’s “Letters from Iwo Jima.”

Fans of the TV series “24” may remember Ellingson as the nephew of Jack Bauer, played by Kiefer Sutherland, during the sixth season. His diverse acting career kicked off with his first role in the 2001 TV movie “Living in Fear,” followed by contributions to “General Hospital,” “Titus,” “Mad TV,” and “Bones.”

Born in Los Angeles and raised in La Verne, California, Evan Ellingson shared his life with three brothers, as detailed in his IMDb profile. The news of his passing has sent shockwaves through the entertainment industry, emphasizing the ongoing struggles faced by individuals battling addiction even after apparent periods of recovery.
